Characteristics of Stress

Definition, types of stress, and the distinction between physical and psychological responses.

Definition Colman (2009): Stress is psychological and physical strain or tension generated by physical, emotional, social, economic or occupational circumstances that are difficult to manage or endure. Two key elements: stress as a physical response and as a psychological response.

Stress as a Physical Response

Hans Selye (1936) identified a universal physical stress response — activation of the nervous system releases hormones including cortisol and adrenaline to mobilise the body to fight or flee.

Stress as a Psychological Response — Transactional Model

Lazarus & Folkman (1984) — Transactional Model
Stress is an interaction between person and environment. Whether we experience stress depends on two stages of appraisal:

Primary appraisal: Is this stressor a threat or significant to me?
Secondary appraisal: Do I have the resources to cope with it?

Weighing both appraisals determines the stress response made.
Evaluation ✔ Explains individual differences — the same stressor causes different stress levels in different people depending on appraisal.
✘ Appraisal processes are difficult to measure objectively — relies heavily on self-report.

Eustress

Selye coined eustress — positive, beneficial stress that motivates. The Yerkes-Dodson curve shows optimal performance at medium arousal; too little or too much arousal impairs performance.
Keller et al. (2012)
Studied 30,000 Americans — high stress was only associated with increased mortality in those who believed stress was harmful. Suggests perception of stress, not stress itself, may determine outcomes.

Types of Stress

Acute

Most common. Short-term response to an immediate threat (exam, near-accident). Ceases once stressor passes. SAM pathway activated then switched off.

Episodic Acute

Repeated bouts of acute stress (e.g. overwork). Can become part of the individual's personality. Harder to treat as person may not recognise it as a problem.

Chronic

Persists long-term — poverty, ongoing relationship problems. Most damaging; prolonged HPA activation leads to immunosuppression and illness.

Biological Explanations of Stress

The SAM pathway (acute stress) and the HPA axis (chronic stress) — the two biological explanations required by the spec.

Fast-acting response. Adrenaline and noradrenaline increase heart rate, blood pressure, divert blood to muscles, dilate bronchioles. Once threat passes, the parasympathetic ANS restores rest-and-digest.

Stressed intensive care workers had thickened arteries — supporting the link between sustained SAM activation and cardiovascular disease (CHD).

✘ Reductionist — ignores the role of cognitive appraisal (Lazarus & Folkman).

Procedure
Measured Natural Killer (NK) cell activity in 75 medical students — one month before exams (low stress) and during the exam period (high stress).
Findings
NK cell activity was significantly lower during exams. Students with highest life stressors showed the most severe immunosuppression.
Conclusion
Psychological stress suppresses immune function via HPA axis and cortisol release.
Kuhlmann et al. (2005)
Administered cortisol to women learning a word list. Those given cortisol showed significantly reduced recall — explains the 'mind goes blank' phenomenon under exam stress.
Evaluation ✔ Objective biological evidence — NK cell count is not reliant on self-report.
✘ Correlational — cannot establish causation. Illness may cause stress rather than stress causing illness (Joel et al., 2008).
✘ Individual differences in cortisol reactivity — people vary considerably in their HPA response to the same stressor.

Social Psychological Explanations

Life events (Holmes & Rahe) and daily hassles (Kanner et al.) — two spec-required social psychological explanations.

Social Psychological Explanation 1 — Life Events (Holmes & Rahe, 1967)

Holmes & Rahe developed the Social Readjustment Rating Scale (SRRS) — 43 life events each assigned a Life Change Unit (LCU) value. Marriage = 50 LCUs baseline; death of spouse = 100. Higher total LCU score = greater risk of stress-related illness. Both positive and negative events included — it is the change itself that causes stress.
Rahe et al. (1970)
Procedure
2,684 US Navy personnel completed the SRE before a tour of duty. Records of illness were kept during the following 6–8 months.
Findings
r = +0.118 — significant positive correlation between LCU score and subsequent illness.
Conclusion
Life changes are a source of stress that increases vulnerability to physical illness.
Evaluation ✘ Correlational — cannot establish cause and effect.
✘ Self-report — Karen Raphael et al. (1991): only 25% of events appeared on both recall lists — very unreliable.
✘ Culture bias — developed in USA; Yan-Ping Zheng & Keh-Ming Lin (1994): different LCU ratings in Chinese sample.
✘ Individual differences — same event affects people very differently; hardy personalities buffer the impact.

Social Psychological Explanation 2 — Daily Hassles (Kanner et al., 1981)

Daily hassles are minor day-to-day irritants (losing keys, traffic, noise). Uplifts are positive daily experiences that may counteract hassles.

Accumulation effect: build-up of hassles → ongoing chronic stress.
Amplification: prior chronic stress depletes coping resources → hassles become harder to deal with.
Kanner et al. (1981)
Procedure
100 participants aged 45–64 completed the Hassles and Uplifts Scale (HSUP) monthly for 9 months.
Findings
Significant negative correlation between hassle frequency and psychological wellbeing. Hassles were a better predictor of wellbeing than life events.
Evaluation ✔ Hassles predict wellbeing better than life events — an important advancement over SRRS.
✘ Self-report bias — social desirability; people may not admit to minor irritants.
✘ Dohrenwend et al. (1984) — 371 clinical psychologists found HSUP items overlap with psychiatric symptoms. The scale may be measuring pre-existing mental illness rather than its causes.

Individual Differences in Stress

Hardiness (Kobasa) and Type A/B personality (Friedman & Rosenman) — two required individual differences explanations.

Individual Differences 1 — Hardiness (Kobasa, 1979)

C

Control

Belief that they can influence what happens — internal locus of control.

C

Commitment

Sense of purpose and meaning; engaged with others and the world.

C

Challenge

See stressors as opportunities for growth; do not expect life to be problem-free.

Kobasa (1979)
Male business executives who had experienced high stress without falling ill scored significantly higher on hardiness measures — they had all 3 Cs.
Maddi (1987) — Bell Telephone
During a period of major redundancies, the third of employees who demonstrated hardy personalities suffered fewer stress-related illnesses (heart problems, depression) than non-hardy colleagues.
Evaluation ✔ Bartone (1999): soldiers with higher hardiness coped better with combat stress — real-world support.
✘ Gender bias — Shepperd (1991): hardiness predicts health for males but findings inconsistent for females.
✘ Sandvik (2013): navy cadets — those low in challenge showed more immune stress responses even when high in control/commitment. All 3 Cs are needed — challenge may be most important.
✘ Funk (1992): overlap with neuroticism — when neuroticism is controlled, the hardiness–illness relationship weakens.

Individual Differences 2 — Type A & B Personality

Type A

  • Competitive, hostile
  • Time-pressured, workaholic
  • Rapid speech and movements
  • Unrealistic sense of urgency
  • Impatient

Type B

  • Relaxed, patient
  • Able to express feelings
  • Calm, flexible
  • Slower speech and movements
  • No unrealistic urgency
Western Collaborative Group Study — Rosenman et al. (1976)
Procedure
Prospective longitudinal study; 3,154 men aged 39–59 assessed for personality type via interview. Followed for 8.5 years.
Findings
257 men developed CHD; 70% were Type A. Type A had higher blood pressure and cholesterol. Risk factors (smoking) were controlled for.
Conclusion
Type A personality is an independent risk factor for coronary heart disease.
Evaluation ✘ Gender and culture bias — all-male US sample. Type A traits overlap with Western masculine ideals (Weidner, 1987).
✘ Confounding variable — interview was conducted provocatively; may have elicited Type A behaviour rather than measuring an existing trait.
✘ Hecker et al. (1988): hostility alone is the most significant predictor of CHD — other Type A components do not significantly increase risk. Type A is not a unified construct.

Methods of Modifying Stress

Beta-blockers, benzodiazepines, SIT and biofeedback — all four required methods with AO1 and full evaluation.

Method 1 — Beta-blockers (e.g. propranolol, atenolol)

Beta-blockers act as antagonists — they block beta-adrenoreceptor sites, preventing adrenaline and noradrenaline from binding. This reduces sympathetic nervous system arousal: lower heart rate and blood pressure.

Non-selective (propranolol): affects heart, kidneys, liver.
Selective (atenolol): affects heart only — fewer systemic effects.
Evaluation ✔ Useful for performance-based acute stress (musicians, surgeons, public speakers) — Lockwood (1989): 27% of major US orchestra musicians used beta-blockers.
✘ Treats symptoms not cause — does not address psychological root of stress.
✘ Off-label use raises ethical/consent issues (Wittich et al., 2012). Banned by IOC in most Olympic sports.
✘ Side effects — dizziness, diarrhoea, cold hands; risk of psychological dependence.

Method 2 — Benzodiazepines (e.g. Librium, Valium)

Enhance the effect of GABA neurotransmitter — increase flow of chloride ions → reduce CNS activity across the whole brain → lower anxiety and stress responses.
Only prescribed for severe stress/anxiety; highly addictive — must be weaned off carefully.
Evaluation ✘ Treats symptoms not cause — dependency means unsuitable for long-term use.
✘ Side effects: drowsiness, impaired judgement, memory problems.

Method 3 — Stress Inoculation Training (Meichenbaum, 1985)

1

Conceptualisation

Therapist and client identify stressors and current coping resources. Stressors broken into manageable components.

2

Skills Acquisition

Learn cognitive and behavioural coping strategies — constructive narrative perspective; rehearse in clinical setting.

3

Application

Practise in increasingly stressful simulated situations. Booster sessions arranged. Relapses treated as learning opportunities.

Sheehy & Horan (2004)
First-year law students received 3 sessions of SIT. Those who received SIT showed significantly lower anxiety and stress over time compared to controls — supports SIT in real-world educational settings.
Evaluation ✔ Treats the cause not just symptoms — changes how stressors are perceived (based on Lazarus & Folkman).
✔ Flexible and individual — tailored to specific stressors; used in medical, military, legal settings.
✘ Moses & Hollandsworth (1985): all three phases are needed — Phase 1 alone was insufficient. Time-consuming and costly.
✘ Robson & Manacapilli (2014): military SIT effective for acute stress but NOT chronic stress (family problems during deployment).

Method 4 — Biofeedback

Client attached to sensors monitoring ANS activity (heart rate, blood pressure, muscle tension, sweat gland activity). Receives real-time signal (light/sound) indicating physiological state. Practises relaxation/meditation to lower arousal — learns to control normally involuntary responses. Can be done via wearable technology and apps at home.
Evaluation ✔ No side effects; no risk of dependency — empowers individual to self-regulate.
✘ Expensive equipment may be unnecessary — relaxation techniques alone may be equally effective.
✘ Artificial lab environment — responses may not generalise to real-life stressors.

Synoptic Links — Issues & Debates

Nature vs Nurture

Biological explanations (SAM/HPA — nature) vs social psychological (life events, hassles — nurture). Individual differences integrate both.

Reductionism

SAM/HPA are biologically reductionist — ignore psychological appraisal. SIT is more holistic, addressing both biological and cognitive factors.

Gender Bias

Stress research historically androcentric — Taylor et al. (2000): 'tend and befriend' female response ignored until recently. Type A study: all male.

Free Will vs Determinism

Biological explanations are deterministic — stress responses are automatic. SIT implies agency — individuals can learn to change their appraisal.

AO1Describe the HPA axis as a biological explanation of stress.[10 marks]
Structure Guide

→ Name the pathway and what triggers it.
→ Describe the sequence: hypothalamus → CRH → pituitary → ACTH → adrenal cortex → cortisol.
→ Explain what cortisol does (at least 3 effects: mobilises glucose, reduces pain, immunosuppression, impairs memory).
→ Include supporting evidence: Kiecolt-Glaser et al. (1984) and/or Kuhlmann et al. (2005).
→ Depth AND range — don't just list, explain each point.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1

The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis is the biological mechanism underlying the response to chronic (long-term) stressors. When a stressor is first encountered, the hypothalamus produces corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH), which signals the pituitary gland to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) into the bloodstream. ACTH then stimulates the adrenal cortex to release cortisol — a steroid hormone in the glucocorticoid family.

Cortisol serves several functions during chronic stress. It mobilises glucose, providing sustained energy for the body to deal with ongoing stressors. It also reduces pain sensitivity, allowing continued functioning. However, cortisol simultaneously suppresses the immune system (immunosuppression) — reducing inflammation and immune responses to divert the body's resources. Whilst adaptive in the short term, prolonged cortisol release leaves the individual increasingly vulnerable to illness and infection.

Additionally, high cortisol levels have been shown to impair memory. Kuhlmann et al. (2005) administered cortisol to women learning a word list and found significantly reduced recall compared to a control group — particularly for negative words. This explains the 'mind goes blank' phenomenon commonly experienced by students during exams. Kiecolt-Glaser et al. (1984) further supported the HPA axis account by measuring Natural Killer (NK) cell activity in 75 medical students, finding significantly reduced NK cell activity during the exam period compared to one month before — demonstrating that academic stress activates the HPA axis to the point of measurable immunosuppression.

Examiner's tip: To reach Band 4, ensure you name the full sequence (CRH → ACTH → cortisol), explain the effects of cortisol rather than just listing them, and include specific study evidence with details.
AO1+AO3Describe and evaluate SIT as a method of modifying stress.[20 marks]
Scenario

Rachel is a newly qualified junior doctor who is finding the demands of her role extremely stressful. She feels overwhelmed by the responsibility of caring for patients, struggles to sleep before shifts, and often feels unable to cope. Her hospital has offered her access to SIT sessions with a clinical psychologist.

Structure Guide — 20 marks (AO1: 10, AO3: 10)

AO1: Three phases (conceptualisation → skills acquisition → application). Theoretical basis (transactional model — changes appraisal). What happens in each phase. Include specific techniques (ABC model, cognitive restructuring, simulated exposure).
AO3: Both strengths AND weaknesses. Use Sheehy & Horan (2004) as support. Moses & Hollandsworth (1985) — all phases needed. Robson & Manacapilli — acute but not chronic. Ethical/social implications. Conclude.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1 — Description (10 marks)

Stress Inoculation Training (SIT), developed by Meichenbaum (1985), is a psychological intervention based on the transactional model of stress (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984). Rather than targeting the physiological stress response directly, SIT aims to change how individuals perceive and appraise stressors — building cognitive and behavioural resilience in the same way a vaccine builds biological immunity by exposing the body to a weakened form of a pathogen.

SIT operates through three overlapping phases. In the Conceptualisation phase, the therapist and client work collaboratively to identify the nature of the client's stressors, their existing coping strategies, and the cognitive appraisals they make. The stressor is broken down into smaller, more manageable components. In Rachel's case, this might involve identifying specific situations — such as breaking bad news or making urgent clinical decisions — as the primary triggers for her stress.

In the Skills Acquisition and Rehearsal phase, the client is taught both cognitive strategies (such as the constructive narrative perspective — restructuring how they think about stressful events) and behavioural coping strategies (relaxation, problem-solving, attention deployment). These are rehearsed in the clinical setting.

In the Application and Follow-through phase, skills are practised in increasingly stressful simulated situations, building resistance to stress through graduated exposure. Booster sessions are arranged, and relapses are treated as learning opportunities rather than failures.

AO3 — Evaluation (10 marks)

A key strength of SIT is that it treats the cause of stress rather than just suppressing its symptoms. By targeting cognitive appraisal — the way Rachel interprets the demands placed on her — SIT produces genuine cognitive change rather than pharmacological suppression. Sheehy and Horan (2004) found that first-year law students who received three sessions of SIT showed significantly lower anxiety and stress over time compared to controls, demonstrating its effectiveness in a real-world professional context analogous to Rachel's situation.

SIT is also flexible and individual — unlike drug therapies, it is tailored to the specific stressors and coping abilities of each client. It has been successfully applied across medical, military and legal settings, suggesting good generalisability across high-stress professions.

However, Moses and Hollandsworth (1985) found that all three phases are needed — participants who received only the conceptualisation phase showed no significant reduction in anxiety. This means SIT is necessarily time-consuming and costly, which may limit its accessibility, particularly for patients under financial pressure or those who cannot commit to multiple sessions.

Robson and Manacapilli (2014) found that military SIT prepared soldiers effectively for acute battlefield stress but left them poorly equipped to deal with chronic stress (e.g. family problems during deployment). For Rachel, whose stress is largely ongoing and chronic, this is a significant limitation — SIT may not address the full nature of her difficulties without additional intervention.

Overall, SIT is one of the most comprehensive psychological approaches to stress management, but its effectiveness is limited by the need for significant time investment and its weaker evidence base for chronic stress. For Rachel, it would be most effective when combined with systemic support from her hospital in reducing unrealistic workload demands — addressing the source of stress, not just the appraisal of it.

AO1Describe the characteristics of stress, including the distinction between eustress and distress. [10][10 marks]

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1 (10 marks)

Colman (2009) defined stress as psychological and physical strain or tension generated by physical, emotional, social, economic or occupational circumstances that are difficult to manage or endure. Stress operates on two levels: as a physical response and as a psychological response.

Hans Selye (1936) identified the physical stress response as a universal activation of the nervous system releasing hormones including adrenaline and cortisol to mobilise the body for fight-or-flight. Lazarus and Folkman (1984) proposed the transactional model to explain the psychological dimension: stress arises from an interaction between the individual and their environment. Whether a stressor produces stress depends on two sequential appraisals — primary appraisal (is this a threat or significant to me?) and secondary appraisal (do I have the resources to cope with it?). If the perceived demand exceeds perceived coping resources, stress is experienced.

Selye distinguished between eustress and distress. Eustress is positive, beneficial stress — the excitement before a performance, competitive pressure that motivates, a stimulating challenge. Distress is negative, damaging stress. The Yerkes-Dodson curve illustrates this relationship: performance improves as arousal increases up to an optimal point, after which further arousal impairs functioning. Too little arousal (boredom) and too much arousal (panic) both reduce performance — only moderate arousal is optimal.

Three types of stress are recognised. Acute stress is the most common — a short-term response to an immediate, identifiable stressor (e.g. an exam, a near-accident). It activates the SAM pathway and ceases once the stressor passes. Episodic acute stress involves repeated bouts of acute stress — the overworked individual constantly in crisis. Chronic stress persists long-term — poverty, ongoing relationship difficulties, caregiving responsibilities. It is the most damaging type: prolonged activation of the HPA axis and sustained cortisol release leads to immunosuppression, cardiovascular damage and cognitive impairment.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO3 (10 marks)

A key strength of the hardiness explanation is real-world support from prospective research. Maddi (1987) studied employees of Illinois Bell Telephone during a period of major redundancies — a genuine, severe chronic stressor. The third of employees who demonstrated higher hardiness scores suffered significantly fewer stress-related illnesses (cardiovascular problems, depression) over the following decade compared to less hardy colleagues. This prospective longitudinal design, using a real occupational stressor rather than a laboratory task, gives the hardiness explanation strong ecological validity. Bartone (1999) provided further support by showing that US soldiers with higher hardiness coped significantly better with the chronic stressors of combat deployment.

However, Shepperd (1991) identified a significant gender bias in hardiness research: the original studies by Kobasa used all-male samples (male business executives), and when hardiness was tested in female samples, its predictive value for health outcomes was considerably less consistent. This limits the generalisability of the explanation to women, who experience many of the same occupational and social stressors as men. Funk (1992) raised a further methodological concern — when neuroticism (a stable personality trait associated with negative affect) was statistically controlled, the relationship between hardiness and illness was substantially weakened, suggesting the hardiness effect may partly reflect the absence of neuroticism rather than the presence of a distinct protective construct.

The Type A/B personality explanation is supported by Rosenman et al.'s (1976) Western Collaborative Group Study — a large-scale, prospective design following 3,154 men over 8.5 years. 70% of the 257 who developed coronary heart disease were classified as Type A, even after controlling for known risk factors such as smoking and cholesterol. The prospective design means personality was assessed before illness developed, strengthening the causal inference.

Nevertheless, Hecker et al. (1988) challenged the validity of the Type A construct itself, finding that hostility was the only component of Type A that significantly predicted CHD — competitiveness and time pressure did not. This suggests Type A as a unified construct lacks discriminant validity. The method of assessment — a structured clinical interview conducted in a deliberately provocative manner — may have induced Type A behaviour rather than measuring a pre-existing stable trait, undermining construct validity. The all-male, American sample also limits the cultural and gender generalisability of the findings.

In conclusion, both explanations offer valuable insights into why the same stressor produces different outcomes in different individuals. However, both are hampered by methodological limitations — gender-biased samples (hardiness), construct validity questions (Type A), and reliance on self-report — that restrict the confidence with which their conclusions can be generalised.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1 (10 marks)

Holmes and Rahe (1967) proposed that stress arises from life events — significant changes in a person's life that require social readjustment. They developed the Social Readjustment Rating Scale (SRRS) by asking 400 participants to rate 43 life events according to the amount of adjustment required. Each event was assigned a Life Change Unit (LCU) value — marriage was used as a baseline of 50 LCUs, with other events rated relative to it. Both positive events (e.g. marriage, promotion) and negative events (e.g. divorce, bereavement) were included — it is the required adjustment, not the emotional valence, that causes stress. A higher total LCU score over a 12-month period was associated with greater risk of stress-related physical illness.

Rahe et al. (1970) tested this in a prospective study of 2,684 US Navy personnel who completed the Schedule of Recent Experiences (SRE) — a life events measure — before a tour of duty. Medical records of illness were kept during the subsequent six to eight months. They found a statistically significant positive correlation of r = +0.118 between LCU scores and illness during the tour — individuals with higher life event scores were more likely to experience illness, supporting Holmes and Rahe's hypothesis.

Kanner et al. (1981) proposed an alternative social psychological explanation based on daily hassles — minor, routine irritants such as losing keys, traffic congestion, noise, or workplace interruptions. They developed the Hassles and Uplifts Scale (HSUP) to measure both the frequency and intensity of daily hassles and uplifts (positive daily experiences). In a longitudinal study of 100 participants aged 45–64, followed monthly for nine months, they found a significant negative correlation between hassle frequency and psychological wellbeing. Two mechanisms are proposed: the accumulation effect — many small hassles accumulate over time to produce chronic stress — and the amplification effect — pre-existing chronic stress depletes coping resources, making each individual hassle harder to manage. Importantly, Kanner et al. found that daily hassles were a better predictor of psychological wellbeing than life events — suggesting that the fabric of everyday experience matters more for health than major life changes.

Characteristics of Addictive Behaviour

Definition, Griffiths' six components, and initiation/maintenance/relapse.

Definition Addiction = compulsive engagement in a behaviour or substance use despite awareness of negative consequences. Can be physical (substance use) or behavioural (gambling, internet use).

Griffiths (1996) — Six Components

Salience

The behaviour dominates thoughts, feelings and activity.

Mood Modification

Using the behaviour to change emotional state — creating a 'buzz' or escape.

Tolerance

Increasing engagement needed to achieve the same effect.

Withdrawal

Unpleasant physical/psychological symptoms when stopped.

Conflict

Interpersonal conflict with others or inner conflict within the individual.

Relapse

Return to the behaviour after a period of abstinence.

Evaluation ✔ Criteria apply beyond substance use — extend to gambling, internet use, and other behavioural addictions.
✘ May be too broad — many people show some criteria without being clinically addicted.

Initiation, Maintenance & Relapse

Initiation

Why the behaviour begins — curiosity, peer pressure, media, genetic vulnerability.

Maintenance

Why it continues — pleasure, tolerance, habit, negative reinforcement (avoiding withdrawal).

Relapse

Return after abstinence — very common. Prochaska & DiClemente (1983): built into the stages of change model as normal.

Biological Explanations

The dopamine hypothesis and the role of addiction genes — two biological explanations required by the Eduqas spec.

Biological Explanation 1 — The Role of Dopamine

The mesolimbic dopamine pathway evolved to reinforce adaptive behaviours (eating, sex). Addictive substances and behaviours produce a far greater dopamine release than natural rewards — hijacking the reward system. This is why drug-induced pleasure far exceeds that of everyday activities.
Stressor / Drug
→
VTA (Ventral Tegmental Area)
→
Nucleus Accumbens (NAc)
→
Intense pleasure / reward
→
Reinforcement → repeated use
Olds & Milner (1954)
Procedure
Electrodes implanted in rats' nucleus accumbens. Rats could press a lever to self-stimulate the reward pathway via electrical current.
Findings
Rats pressed the lever up to 7,000 times/hour, choosing brain stimulation over food and water — even when starved.
Conclusion
The mesolimbic dopamine system is the biological basis of reward. Addictive substances hijack this same pathway — producing a reward signal far more powerful than anything the brain evolved to handle.

Tolerance, D2 Receptors & Withdrawal

D2 receptors are located on the postsynaptic neuron in the nucleus accumbens. When dopamine binds to D2, the pleasure signal fires. With repeated drug use, the brain reduces the number and sensitivity of D2 receptors (like turning down a volume control that's been blasted at full volume).

Tolerance: Fewer, less sensitive D2 receptors → need more drug for same effect.
Withdrawal: Without the drug, the blunted reward system cannot generate normal pleasure from everyday activities — flat mood, craving, anhedonia.
Volkow et al. (1997) — PET scans
Findings
PET brain imaging showed a measurable decrease in D2 receptor density in the nucleus accumbens of cocaine, heroin and alcohol addicts compared to non-addicts. This is objective, observable evidence of tolerance at the biological level.

The Frontal Cortex & Maintenance of Addiction

Repeated drug use causes the dopamine system to attribute high salience (importance) to drug-related cues — people, places, smells, music associated with past use. This competes with the frontal cortex (responsible for self-control and rational decision-making).

Eventually, frontal cortex functioning is impaired — addicts want to stop but the brain circuits controlling impulse inhibition are damaged, making cessation neurologically difficult.
Volkow et al. (1992)
Cocaine addicts showed abnormal frontal cortex activity and performed significantly worse on decision-making tasks than non-addicts — directly linking addiction-related brain changes to impaired self-control.
Wang et al. (1999)
Found increased frontal cortex activity in addicts when exposed to drug-related cues — demonstrating attentional bias towards addiction-related stimuli. This explains cue-induced craving and why environments associated with past use trigger powerful urges to use again.
Evaluation ✔ Objective brain imaging evidence — PET scans (Volkow et al.) provide observable, measurable data not reliant on self-report. Highly replicable.
✔ Explains both tolerance AND withdrawal via a single mechanism (D2 receptor desensitisation). Also explains why stopping is so hard (frontal cortex impairment).
✘ Reductionist — reduces a complex behaviour to one neurotransmitter. Ignores psychological (cognitive biases, SLT) and social factors (peers, media).
✘ Cause or effect? Fewer D2 receptors may be a pre-existing biological vulnerability (not caused by drug use) — Blum et al. (1990) found DRD2 A1 allele in 69% of alcoholics suggesting genetic origin.
✘ Animal studies (Olds & Milner) — ethical concerns (invasive surgery without consent); rats cannot show complex human cognitive patterns around addiction.

Biological Explanation 2 — Addiction Genes

Genes may make some individuals more biologically vulnerable to addiction by affecting how they respond to substances and how their brain processes reward. Addiction is polygenic — no single gene causes it, but several genes affecting the dopamine system increase risk.

DRD2 Gene

Variants of DRD2 are linked to fewer D2 receptors. Individuals with the DRD2 A1 allele have a less responsive reward system and may seek substances to boost dopamine to a normal level.

Blum et al. (1990): DRD2 A1 allele found in 69% of deceased alcoholics vs 20% of controls.
Comings et al. (1996): DRD2 A1 allele in 49% of compulsive gamblers vs 26% of controls — extends the gene-addiction link to behavioural addictions.

Diathesis-Stress Model

Genes alone do not cause addiction. A genetic predisposition is triggered by environmental factors — stress, trauma, peer pressure, media. This explains why not everyone with a genetic risk becomes addicted, and why environmental interventions still work for genetically vulnerable individuals.

The model integrates nature and nurture — the most complete biological account of addiction.

Kendler et al. (1992) — Female Twin Study
Sample
1,030 female twin pairs. Structured diagnostic interviews on alcohol dependence.
Findings
MZ concordance significantly higher than DZ. Heritability of alcohol dependence estimated at ~50–60%.
Limitation
All-female sample — cannot generalise to males. True et al. (1999) addressed this — 3,372 male veteran twin pairs; estimated nicotine dependence heritability at 61% (MZ 64–79% vs DZ 40–49%).
Evaluation ✔ Twin studies provide the strongest design for separating genetic from environmental contributions.
✔ Comings et al. (1996) extends gene evidence to behavioural addictions (gambling) — not just substance use.
✘ MZ concordance below 100% — environment matters too; confirms diathesis-stress model.
✘ Polygenic — no single addiction gene identified; very difficult to isolate specific gene effects in complex human behaviour.
✘ Deterministic implications — if addiction is genetically predetermined, this raises ethical questions about individual responsibility and criminal justice treatment of addicts.

Individual Differences Explanations

Personality (Eysenck's theory) and cognitive biases — two required individual differences explanations.

Individual Differences 1 — Personality (Eysenck's Theory)

Extraversion (E)

Lower cortical arousal — seeks external stimulation. More likely to use substances to reach optimal arousal level.

Neuroticism (N)

Emotionally unstable and anxiety-prone. May self-medicate to manage negative emotional states.

Psychoticism (P)

Impulsive, aggressive, unconcerned with consequences. Strongest predictor of substance use and addiction.

Dong et al. (2013)
3,557 Chinese university students — those with internet addiction disorder scored significantly higher on N and P, and lower on conscientiousness than non-addicts.
McMurran et al. (2010)
Impulsive rats were significantly more likely to self-administer cocaine than non-impulsive rats — supporting a biological link between impulsivity (high P) and addiction vulnerability.
Evaluation ✔ Supported across multiple addiction types (substance and behavioural).
✘ Correlational — personality may change as a result of addiction, not cause it.
✘ ASPD also strongly linked to addiction (Hesselbrock & Hesselbrock, 1992) — personality alone cannot explain all cases.

Individual Differences 2 — Cognitive Biases

Cognitive biases are systematic errors in thinking that lead individuals to overestimate rewards and underestimate risks of addictive behaviour. Particularly important in gambling addiction.

Illusion of Control

Belief that skill influences random outcomes — using 'lucky' numbers, rituals, specific strategies in games of chance.

Representativeness

Gambler's fallacy — 'I've lost 5 times in a row so a win is due.' Belief that random events form patterns.

Availability Heuristic

Wins are vivid and memorable; losses are not. Casinos design environments to make wins highly visible.

Hindsight Bias

After an outcome, gamblers believe they 'knew' it would happen — reinforces false sense of control and predictability.

Griffiths (1994) — Thinking Aloud Study
Procedure
30 regular and 30 non-regular gamblers on a fruit machine task. Participants asked to 'think aloud' — verbalisations recorded.
Findings
Regular gamblers produced significantly more irrational verbalisations — e.g. 'the machine is in a good mood today'.
Evaluation ✔ Ecologically valid method — actual gambling behaviour rather than hypothetical self-report.
✔ Practical application: cognitive restructuring in CBT targets and challenges these irrational beliefs directly.
✘ Biases may be a consequence of addiction, not a cause — causality unclear.
✘ Mainly relevant to gambling — less applicable to substance addictions.

Social Psychological Explanations

Peer influences (SLT and social norms) and the role of the media — two required social psychological explanations.

Social Psychological Explanation 1 — Peer Influences

Bandura (1977) — SLT: Behaviour is learned through observation and imitation. Observing peers being rewarded for substance use (socially accepted, relaxed) increases likelihood of imitation via vicarious reinforcement. Low self-efficacy to resist peer pressure further increases risk of initiation.
Social norms: Descriptive norms (what peers actually do) and injunctive norms (what is socially approved) both influence behaviour. Students consistently overestimate how much peers drink — this inflated norm increases their own consumption.
Simon-Morton & Farhat (2010)
Reviewed 40 prospective studies — consistent positive relationship between peer drug/alcohol engagement and adolescent initiation.
Clayton et al. (2007)
Peer norms and peer behaviour were the strongest predictors of alcohol use in US college students — stronger than parental influence or individual attitudes.
Evaluation ✔ Strong prospective evidence — peer influence predicts future addiction, not just current use.
✔ Practical application: social norms marketing campaigns in schools/universities.
✘ Peer selection effect — people with existing tendencies may seek out drug-using peers (bidirectional).
✘ Not all people with drug-using peers become addicted — individual differences mediate the effect.

Social Psychological Explanation 2 — Role of the Media

Media portrays addictive behaviours as glamorous, exciting and consequence-free. Applying SLT: media figures act as role models — viewers vicariously experience perceived rewards without consequences. Two key areas: entertainment media (films/TV) and advertising (gambling/alcohol).
Dal Cin et al. (2008)
Longitudinal study of 2,346 non-smoking adolescents. Greater exposure to smoking in films significantly predicted initiation at 2-year follow-up — even after controlling for other risk factors.
Derevensky et al. (2010)
Gambling advertising significantly increased positive attitudes towards gambling and lowered perceived risk — particularly in younger males.
Evaluation ✔ Consistent evidence across tobacco, alcohol and gambling. Practical: ASA restrictions on gambling adverts (watershed rules).
✘ Mainly correlational — cannot establish that media directly causes addiction.
✘ Media interacts with pre-existing personality and peer factors — not a standalone cause.

Methods of Modifying Addictive Behaviour

Agonist/antagonist substitution and aversion therapy — two required methods with full AO1 and evaluation.

Method 1 — Agonist & Antagonist Substitution

Agonist

Mimics the addictive substance at the same receptors but in a safer, controlled, medically supervised way. Reduces withdrawal and craving.

Example: Methadone (for heroin). Oral liquid, given under pharmacy supervision. Avoids injection risks (HIV, hepatitis).

Antagonist

Blocks receptor sites — prevents the addictive substance from having its rewarding effect. Eliminates positive reinforcement.

Example: Naltrexone (opioid/alcohol). Blocks opioid receptors — heroin produces no euphoria if taken. Available as tablet, injection, or implant.

NICE (2007)
Assessed 31 systematic reviews — methadone significantly better than placebo for reducing heroin use and criminal activity. Recommended as first-line pharmacological treatment.
Volpicelli et al. (1992)
Double-blind RCT of naltrexone for alcohol dependence — naltrexone significantly reduced relapse rates and number of drinking days compared to placebo.
Evaluation ✔ Methadone reduces harm even without full abstinence — pragmatic, evidence-based.
✔ Naltrexone: no abuse potential — not addictive or rewarding. Implant form improves compliance.
✘ Methadone is itself addictive; compliance only 54% (Rettenbacher et al.). ~£15,000 per patient/year.
✘ Naltrexone: high oral dropout rates — no physical dependence on the drug so motivation is low.
✘ Neither method addresses psychological causes of addiction.

Method 2 — Aversion Therapy

Uses classical conditioning to create a negative conditioned response to the addictive stimulus.

UCS: Aversive stimulus (nausea-inducing drug, electric shock).
CS: Addictive substance/behaviour.
UCR: Nausea/discomfort.
CR (goal): After repeated pairings, the addictive stimulus alone produces aversion — replacing the pleasurable CR.

Antabuse (Disulfiram) — Alcohol

Daily tablet that causes intense nausea, vomiting, palpitations and flushing when alcohol is consumed. Diehl et al. (2010): supervised Antabuse significantly more effective than self-administered — compliance is the critical variable.

Rapid Smoking — Nicotine

Patient smokes continuously and rapidly until intensely nauseous. Hajek & Stead (2013): Cochrane review — rapid smoking produced significantly better quit rates than controls at 6 months.

Evaluation ✔ Grounded in well-established classical conditioning principles.
✔ Most effective when combined with CBT — addresses conditioned response AND underlying cognitions.
✘ Extinction — conditioned aversion weakens over time; relapse common when treatment ends.
✘ Does not address underlying cause — symptom substitution may occur.
✘ Ethical concerns — deliberate use of painful/distressing stimuli; valid consent issues.
✘ Compliance: individuals can simply stop taking Antabuse whenever they want to drink.

AO1+AO2+AO3Describe and evaluate cognitive biases as an explanation for addictive behaviour. Apply your knowledge to the scenario. [25]
Scenario

Mia is a 30-year-old who gambles regularly on online slot machines. After a run of losses, she feels certain she is 'about to win'. She has specific rituals she performs when playing — always using the same username and starting at the same time of day. Despite significant financial losses, Mia believes she will 'make her money back if she just plays long enough.'

Structure Guide — 25 marks (AO1:10, AO2:5, AO3:10)

AO1 (10): Define cognitive biases + Kahneman & Tversky (1972). Describe all four biases: illusion of control, representativeness, availability heuristic, hindsight bias. Griffiths (1994) thinking aloud study — findings.
AO2 (5): Apply each bias explicitly to Mia — name the bias, then link to her specific behaviour.
AO3 (10): Strength: Griffiths — ecologically valid. Strength: CBT application. Weakness: cause or effect. Weakness: mainly gambling, not all addictions. Conclude.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1 — Description (10 marks)

Cognitive biases are systematic errors in thinking that lead individuals to make predictable, irrational decisions. In the context of addiction, particularly gambling, biases cause people to overestimate the rewards and underestimate the risks of continued engagement. Kahneman and Tversky (1972) demonstrated that humans routinely use cognitive heuristics (mental shortcuts) when making decisions, and that these shortcuts produce consistent, systematic errors in risk assessment.

The illusion of control (Langer, 1975) is the belief that personal skill or ritual can influence a random outcome. Gamblers use lucky usernames, specific betting patterns or rituals, believing these give them an advantage in games of pure chance. The representativeness bias (gambler's fallacy) is the belief that random events follow patterns — after a long losing streak, gamblers believe a win is statistically 'due', even though each spin is an independent event. The availability heuristic means wins are more vivid and emotionally memorable than losses, leading gamblers to overestimate winning frequency. Casinos and online platforms deliberately make wins visible and celebratory to exploit this. Hindsight bias leads gamblers to believe they 'knew' an outcome would occur after the fact, reinforcing a false sense of control and predictability.

Griffiths (1994) investigated cognitive biases using a 'thinking aloud' method with 30 regular and 30 non-regular gamblers on a fruit machine. Regular gamblers produced significantly more irrational verbalisations — for example 'the machine is in a good mood today' — demonstrating that problem gamblers actively construct false cognitive frameworks to explain random events.

AO2 — Application (5 marks)

Mia's behaviour directly illustrates all four cognitive biases. Her belief that she is 'about to win' after a run of losses is a textbook example of the representativeness bias — she incorrectly interprets independent random events as forming a pattern, believing past losses make a future win more probable. Her rituals (same username, same time of day) demonstrate the illusion of control — she believes personal behaviours can influence the outcome of a random computer algorithm. Her belief that she will 'make her money back if she plays long enough' combines the availability heuristic (wins are more memorable than equivalent losses) with a distorted perception of probability. These biases collectively maintain Mia's gambling despite clear evidence of harm.

AO3 — Evaluation (10 marks)

A key strength is that the cognitive bias explanation is supported by direct experimental evidence. Griffiths' (1994) thinking aloud method provided objective, qualitative data on the irrational thought processes of problem gamblers — directly observing cognitive distortions rather than relying solely on self-report. The method has good ecological validity as it studied actual gambling behaviour.

The explanation also has important practical value. Identifying cognitive biases provides the basis for cognitive restructuring in CBT — directly challenging and correcting irrational beliefs. This gives the explanation clear clinical utility, particularly for gambling addiction treatment.

However, a significant weakness is that cognitive biases may be a consequence of addiction rather than a cause. An individual who gambles heavily may develop increasingly distorted thinking as addiction progresses. Cross-sectional research cannot determine whether biased thinking preceded or followed problematic gambling — limiting causal conclusions.

The explanation is also somewhat incomplete — it focuses primarily on gambling and does not account for substance addictions equally. Substance addictions involve profound neurological changes (Volkow et al., 1997) that cannot be explained by cognitive biases alone. A full explanation requires an integrative account incorporating biological, individual difference and social factors. Overall, cognitive biases are a well-evidenced explanation for gambling addiction specifically, but their scope is narrower than a biological or social learning account.

Characteristics of Schizophrenia

Definition, positive and negative symptoms, disordered thinking, and issues with diagnosis.

DefinitionA severe psychotic disorder characterised by loss of touch with reality. Affects ~1 in 100 people. Diagnosed using ICD-10 (UK) or DSM-5 (USA). Differences between criteria can affect diagnosis rates and cross-cultural generalisability.

Positive Symptoms (Type 1) — Additional Experiences

Hallucinations

Perceptions without external stimuli. Auditory most common — hearing voices. Sommer et al. (2009): 60–80% of people with schizophrenia hear voices.

Delusions

Fixed false beliefs despite contradictory evidence. Types: persecution (being watched), grandeur (special powers), reference (events refer specifically to them).

Disorganised Thinking

Derailment (loosely connected thoughts), word salad (incoherent speech). Individual often unaware how their speech sounds.

Psychomotor Disturbance

Catatonia — remaining in unusual postures for long periods. Childlike behaviour or unpredictable agitation.

Negative Symptoms (Type 2) — Reductions from Normal Functioning

Alogia

Poverty of speech — very little, empty or brief responses.

Avolition

Absence of motivation — unable to initiate or sustain goal-directed behaviour.

Affective Flattening

Reduced emotional expression — flat voice tone, limited facial expression.

Anhedonia

Inability to experience pleasure from normally enjoyable activities.

Additional Features & Diagnostic Issues

Depression: Many experience depression alongside symptoms — may be a reaction to having the disorder, or part of the condition itself.
Lack of insight: Many do not believe their hallucinations or delusions are symptoms of a disorder — making accepting diagnosis and treatment very difficult.
Stigma: Schizophrenia is widely misunderstood. Media often portrays it as violent — the 'split personality' label is inaccurate and harmful.
Rosenhan (1973) — 'On Being Sane in Insane Places'
Procedure
8 pseudo-patients presented to 12 US psychiatric hospitals reporting one symptom: hearing a voice say 'thud'. All other behaviour was completely normal.
Findings
All 8 admitted. 7 diagnosed with schizophrenia. Average stay 19 days. Normal behaviour recorded as symptoms ('writing behaviour observed'). Discharged as 'schizophrenia in remission'.
Conclusion
Once labelled, everything is interpreted through that diagnostic lens. Raises profound concerns about the validity and reliability of psychiatric diagnosis.

Biological Explanations

The dopamine hypothesis and structural abnormalities — two required biological explanations.

Biological Explanation 1 — The Dopamine Hypothesis

Original Hypothesis

Excess dopamine in the mesolimbic pathway → positive symptoms. Evidence: antipsychotics (block D2) reduce positive symptoms. Amphetamines (↑ dopamine) induce psychosis.

Revised Hypothesis

Mesolimbic: over-activity → positive symptoms.
Mesocortical: under-activity (VTA → frontal lobe) → negative symptoms and cognitive deficits.

Psychiatric Genomics Consortium (2014)
International study — 108 genome locations associated with schizophrenia, many linked to dopamine regulation — provides a genetic basis for dopamine imbalances.
Evaluation✔ Strong pharmacological support — D2-blocking drugs reliably reduce positive symptoms (replicated thousands of times).
✘ Atypical antipsychotics also affect serotonin — dopamine alone cannot fully explain schizophrenia.
✘ Cause or effect? Dopamine changes may result from schizophrenia rather than cause it (Davis et al., 1991 — PET scans).

CAT and MRI scans showed significantly enlarged ventricles in people with schizophrenia compared to controls — particularly pronounced in males (Andreasen).
Vita et al. (1988) & Luchins et al. (1979)
Vita: 20–35% of those with chronic schizophrenia showed cortical atrophy — linked to negative symptoms. Luchins: reversed cerebral asymmetry in 20% of Sz patients vs 5% of controls — links to language disturbances.

✘ Not universal — many people with Sz show no structural differences at all (McGrath et al., 2004).
✘ Long-term antipsychotic use may itself cause structural changes — cannot establish whether abnormalities are cause or consequence.
✘ Neurodevelopmental vs neurodegenerative debate — abnormalities may be present from birth (Marenco et al., 2003) or develop after onset.

Individual Differences Explanations

The psychodynamic approach — Fromm-Reichmann's schizophrenogenic mother, double bind theory, and Freudian concepts.

Psychodynamic Explanation — Fromm-Reichmann (1948)

Schizophrenogenic mother: A cold, rejecting, overprotective and domineering mother who sends contradictory messages to the child. The child becomes confused and distressed, retreating into schizophrenia as a defence mechanism.

Double bind theory (Bateson et al., 1956): Contradictory messages sent simultaneously — e.g. saying 'I love you' with cold, rejecting body language. The child cannot respond in any way that satisfies the parent — trapped in an irresolvable communicative situation.
Freudian concepts — Fixation & Regression:
Fixation at the oral stage (from birth) is associated with schizophrenia. When faced with adult difficulties, the ego regresses to this primitive state where reality is not well developed. Unrestrained id impulses produce hallucinations, delusions and loss of reality contact.
Evaluation ✘ Unfalsifiable — Freudian concepts cannot be tested by scientific methods.
✘ No effective treatment — Stroup et al. (1997): psychoanalytic treatment no better than no treatment for Sz. CBT and antipsychotics have far stronger evidence.
✘ Inconsistent support — Jacobsen & Jacobsen (1994): 50 controls also had a schizophrenogenic mother yet did not develop Sz.
✘ Ignores genetics — Gottesman & Shields: MZ concordance ~48% vs DZ ~17%; genetic factors play a major role entirely ignored by psychodynamic approach.
✘ Gender bias — places full responsibility for schizophrenia on the mother — androcentric and stigmatising.

Social Psychological Explanations

Dysfunctional families (double bind & expressed emotion) and sociocultural factors (urbanicity, isolation, ethnicity).

Social Psychological Explanation 1 — Dysfunctional Families

Expressed Emotion (EE): The emotional atmosphere within a family — specifically the amount of critical comments, hostility and emotional over-involvement directed at the person with schizophrenia. High EE environments significantly increase relapse rates.
Brown et al. (1958)
156 male patients discharged from hospital — those returning to high-EE homes were significantly more likely to relapse than those returning to low-EE homes.
Vaughn & Leff (1976)
51% relapse rate in high-EE homes vs 13% in low-EE homes over 9 months. More than 35 hours/week contact with high-EE relative was particularly predictive of relapse.
Evaluation✔ Quantitative data — strong, replicable support. Directly supports family therapy as treatment.
✘ Cause or effect? High EE may develop in response to the patient's difficult behaviour — direction of causality unclear.
✘ Shared environment or shared genes? High-EE families may have schizophrenia traits themselves — genetic confound.
✘ Explains relapse NOT onset — cannot explain why schizophrenia first develops.

Social Psychological Explanation 2 — Sociocultural Factors

Urbanicity

Higher prevalence of Sz in urban than rural areas. Van Os et al. (2005): 7,076 participants — urban environment significantly predicted Sz diagnosis. Social drift vs environmental cause debate.

Social Isolation

Living in isolation associated with higher Sz risk. Attermann et al. (1993): individuals who developed Sz were more likely to have been socially isolated and introverted in earlier life.

Ethnicity & Discrimination

African-Caribbean individuals diagnosed at disproportionately higher rates in UK. Harrison et al. (1988); Bhugra et al. (1997): discrimination in services is a key factor.

Evaluation✔ Mortensen et al. (2000): birth in urban area is a risk factor — supports environmental causation, not just drift.
✘ Social isolation may be an early prodromal symptom rather than a cause.
✘ Difficult to separate experience of discrimination from possible genetic vulnerability — diathesis-stress model.

Methods of Modifying Behaviour

Antipsychotic drugs (conventional and atypical) and CBT — both required with full AO1 and evaluation.

Method 1 — Antipsychotic Drugs

Conventional (e.g. chlorpromazine)

First generation (1950s). Block D2 receptors — dopamine antagonists. Reduce positive symptoms. Cole et al. (1964): 75% improved vs 25% placebo in NIMH study.

Side effects: Tardive dyskinesia (involuntary movements), Parkinsonism.

Atypical (e.g. clozapine)

Second generation (1990s). Block D2 AND serotonin (5-HT2A) receptors. Better for negative symptoms. Seeman (2002) 'fast-off' theory: bind loosely to D2 → fewer motor side effects.

Clozapine: 3% risk of agranulocytosis — weekly blood tests required. Ravanic et al. (2009): 0.9 adverse events vs 2.7 for haloperidol over 5 years.

Evaluation✔ Lawrie (2011): antipsychotics transformed care from institutionalisation to community living.
✘ Non-compliance: Rettenbacher — full compliance only 54.2%; 37.5% fully non-compliant.
✘ Valid consent issues — patients in acute episodes may lack capacity. Szasz: 'chemical straitjacket'.
✘ Tiihonen et al. (2006): 37-fold increase in suicide in patients who stopped medication; NCISH (2015): 29% of those who committed homicide were non-adherent.

Method 2 — Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)

1. Engagement

Build rapport; explore symptoms and coping. May have had negative psychiatric experiences.

2. Psycho-education

Normalise psychotic symptoms; offer alternative explanations; increase client's own understanding.

3. Cognitive Strategies

ABC model (verbal challenge); thought diary; behavioural experiments (e.g. music to reduce voices).

4. Behavioural Skills

Relaxation, activity scheduling, distraction — for residual symptoms not managed by medication.

5. Relapse Prevention

Identify early warning signs; plan what client and family would do if symptoms return.

Key Study

Kuipers et al. (1997): 60 medication-resistant patients — 50% of CBT group improved vs 31% standard care only (RCT).

Evaluation✔ RCT evidence — Kuipers et al. CBT significantly better than medication alone for medication-resistant patients.
✔ Empowers clients — uses word 'client' not 'patient'; clients generally satisfied (Kuipers, 1997).
✘ Jauhar et al. (2014) meta-analysis: only small effect from CBT on schizophrenia symptoms.
✘ Tarrier et al. (2004): same relapse rate as standard care at 18-month follow-up — short-term benefits only.
✘ Access inequality: NHS Trusts offer CBT to only 14–67% of patients (NICE recommends all adults with psychosis — National Audit of Schizophrenia, 2013).

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1 — Description (10 marks)

Schizophrenia is a severe psychotic disorder characterised by a loss of touch with reality. It affects approximately 1 in 100 people and is diagnosed using either the ICD-10 (used in the UK) or DSM-5 (USA). The DSM-5 requires at least two symptoms for ≥1 month, with at least one being hallucinations, delusions or disorganised speech, accompanied by significant social or occupational impairment.

Positive symptoms are additional experiences not present in healthy individuals. Hallucinations are perceptions without external stimuli — auditory hallucinations (hearing voices) are most common, affecting 60–80% of those with schizophrenia (Sommer et al., 2009). Delusions are fixed false beliefs held despite contradictory evidence — persecution delusions (belief of being monitored), grandeur delusions, and reference delusions (belief that events are specifically directed at oneself). Disorganised thinking involves incoherent or loosely connected speech — individuals are often unaware how their speech sounds to others.

Negative symptoms represent reductions from normal functioning. Alogia is poverty of speech — very brief, empty responses to questions. Avolition is a lack of motivation to initiate or sustain goal-directed behaviour. Affective flattening involves reduced emotional expression. Anhedonia is the inability to experience pleasure. A particularly important additional feature is lack of insight — many people with schizophrenia do not believe their experiences are symptoms of a disorder, making accepting diagnosis and treatment extremely difficult.

AO2 — Application (5 marks)

Sam's presentation maps clearly onto both positive and negative symptom categories. His belief that his tutors are 'watching him through his phone camera' is a persecution delusion, and his belief that thoughts have been 'placed in his head' is thought insertion — a positive symptom where external forces are believed to insert thoughts into one's mind. His behaviour of 'talking to himself, laughing or looking frightened for no apparent reason' is consistent with auditory hallucinations — he is responding to voices others cannot hear.

Sam also shows several negative symptoms. His brief one-word answers demonstrate alogia, his withdrawal from university and failure to leave his bedroom suggests avolition, and 'showing little emotion' indicates affective flattening. His withdrawal from university also illustrates the significant occupational and social impairment required for a DSM-5 diagnosis.

AO1+AO3Analyse and evaluate dysfunctional families as a social psychological explanation of schizophrenia. [20]
Structure Guide — 20 marks (AO1: 10, AO3: 10)

AO1: Double bind (Bateson et al.) — mechanism. Expressed emotion — definition, components (critical comments, hostility, EOI). Brown et al. (1958) findings. Vaughn & Leff (1976) relapse stats. Camberwell Family Interview.
AO3: Strength: quantitative support (Vaughn & Leff). Strength: practical application — family therapy. Weakness: cause or effect (reverse causality). Weakness: shared genes confound. Weakness: explains relapse not onset. Conclude.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1

Bateson et al. (1956) proposed the double bind theory — schizophrenia develops when a child is repeatedly exposed to contradictory messages from a parent simultaneously. For example, a mother may verbally say 'I love you' whilst showing cold, rejecting body language. The child cannot respond in any way that satisfies the parent and becomes trapped in an irresolvable communicative situation, which Bateson associated with the development of schizophrenia.

Expressed emotion (EE) refers to the emotional atmosphere within a family — specifically the degree of critical comments, hostility and emotional over-involvement (EOI) directed at the individual with schizophrenia. George Brown joined the MRC Social Psychiatry Unit in 1956 and developed the concept, measured via the Camberwell Family Interview (CFI) — a lengthy structured interview assessing five components: critical comments, hostility, EOI, warmth and positive remarks. Brown et al. (1958) studied 156 male patients discharged from hospital, finding those returning to high-EE homes were significantly more likely to relapse. Vaughn and Leff (1976) quantified this: 51% relapse rate in high-EE homes vs 13% in low-EE homes over 9 months. Leff and Vaughn (1985) further found that more than 35 hours per week of direct contact with a high-EE relative was particularly predictive of relapse.

AO3

A key strength is the strong quantitative empirical support. Vaughn and Leff's stark finding (51% vs 13% relapse) provides compelling, measurable evidence that the family emotional environment directly impacts schizophrenia outcomes. This finding has been replicated across multiple cultures, giving it good external validity. Furthermore, the EE explanation directly supports family therapy as a treatment — Leff et al. (1982) demonstrated that family therapy designed to reduce EE significantly decreased relapse rates compared to medication alone, validating the explanation through its practical application.

However, a fundamental weakness is the cause and effect problem. It is difficult to establish whether dysfunctional family patterns cause schizophrenia or develop as a response to living with someone who has it. Having a family member with schizophrenia — who may behave unpredictably, require constant monitoring, and place enormous demands on caregivers — is highly stressful. High EE may therefore be a consequence of schizophrenia rather than its cause. This is a significant limitation as it questions the entire direction of effect assumed by the explanation.

A further limitation is the shared genes confound. Families with a high genetic loading for schizophrenia may also display high-EE communication styles because the parents themselves carry schizophrenia traits. What appears to be an environmental family effect may actually reflect shared genetic vulnerability — making it very difficult to disentangle family environment from genetic predisposition. Additionally, the EE explanation only accounts for relapse, not onset — it cannot explain why schizophrenia first develops. A complete explanation of schizophrenia must account for both initial onset and subsequent course, making this explanation necessarily incomplete on its own.

Cultural Bias in Psychology

The tendency of psychological research to reflect the norms, values and assumptions of Western culture — and how this distorts our understanding of human behaviour.

The Controversy The majority of psychological research is conducted in WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich and Democratic) societies, yet findings are presented as universal truths about all humans. This is controversial because psychology may not accurately represent the full diversity of human experience across cultures.

Types of Cultural Bias

Ethnocentrism

Judging other cultures by the norms of your own. Western psychology assumes its norms are universal. Example: diagnosing behaviour as abnormal when it is culturally normative in another society (e.g. hearing the voices of ancestors — normal in some cultures, labelled as psychosis in Western diagnostic systems).

Alpha Bias

Exaggerating cultural differences — treating cultures as monolithic and stereotyped rather than acknowledging within-group variation.

Beta Bias

Ignoring or minimising cultural differences — applying findings from one cultural group to all others without justification. E.g. applying the SRRS (Holmes & Rahe) to non-American populations as if the same events carry the same stress value.

The WEIRD Problem

Henrich et al. (2010): 96% of psychology research in top journals used Western samples; 68% used American samples. Yet Western populations are only ~12% of the world. Any "universal" conclusion from this sample has extremely limited validity.

Cultural Bias in Component 3 Research

Yan-Ping Zheng & Keh-Ming Lin (1994) — Stress & Life Events
Study
Administered the SRRS (Holmes & Rahe, 1967) to a Chinese sample.
Findings
Significantly different LCU ratings for the same life events compared to American norms. Events involving interpersonal shame and loss of face were rated far more stressful in the Chinese sample.
Conclusion
The SRRS reflects American cultural values — it cannot validly be applied cross-culturally without modification. This is beta bias in stress research.
Harrison et al. (1988) — Over-Diagnosis in Schizophrenia
Finding
African-Caribbean individuals in Nottingham were diagnosed with schizophrenia at disproportionately higher rates than white British individuals.
Explanation
DSM and ICD were developed in Western cultural contexts and may not capture culturally specific expressions of distress. Clinician bias and cultural differences in expressing beliefs may both contribute. This is ethnocentrism in diagnostic practice.
Becker et al. (2002) — Media & Eating Disorders in Fiji
After the introduction of Western television to Fiji (1995), disordered eating behaviours rose dramatically in adolescent girls. Demonstrates that Western media norms can distort behaviour in non-Western cultures — but also shows the limits of generalising Western addiction/media findings globally.
Evaluation ✔ Cultural bias critiques have driven genuine improvements — DSM-5 now includes a Cultural Formulation Interview; NICE guidelines increasingly reference cultural considerations.
✔ Cross-cultural research (Buss, 1989 — mate preferences in 37 cultures) has found some universal psychological patterns — suggesting not all psychology is culturally relative. Some aspects of stress and addiction may be genuinely universal.
✘ Even culturally aware researchers face practical constraints — global representative sampling is expensive and logistically extremely difficult. Awareness of WEIRD bias does not automatically solve it.
✘ Cultural relativism taken too far would mean no behaviour can be judged across cultures — including genuinely harmful practices. Some argue there are universal psychological rights that transcend cultural context.
✘ Individual differences within cultures are often larger than differences between cultures — treating culture as a homogeneous variable risks its own form of stereotyping (alpha bias).

Use of Non-Human Animals in Psychological Research

The ethical and scientific debate around using animals in psychology — with specific reference to Component 3 studies.

The Controversy Animal research has produced some of psychology's most important findings (Pavlov's conditioning; Olds & Milner's reward pathway). Yet it raises profound ethical questions about causing suffering to sentient beings who cannot consent. The debate centres on whether scientific and practical benefits justify the harm caused to animals.

Animal Studies Directly in Component 3

Olds & Milner (1954) — Addiction

Rats with electrodes in the nucleus accumbens self-stimulated 7,000x/hour. Foundational evidence for the mesolimbic dopamine reward pathway. Required invasive brain surgery on rats — no consent possible, likely distress and pain.

McMurran et al. (2010) — Addiction

Impulsive rats were significantly more likely to self-administer cocaine. Required deliberately exposing rats to an addictive substance. Raises the question of deliberately inducing addiction and dependency in animals for research purposes.

Arguments FOR Using Animals in Psychology

Scientific necessity: Some research cannot be ethically conducted on humans — deliberately inducing addiction, implanting electrodes, creating brain lesions. Animal research provides causal data impossible to obtain otherwise.

Evolutionary continuity (Darwin): Humans share nervous systems and brain structures with other mammals. Olds & Milner's rat NAc findings translated directly into understanding human dopamine addiction and developing methadone and naltrexone treatments.

Medical benefits: Animal research has contributed to antipsychotic drugs, addiction treatments, and stress-management therapies — potentially benefiting millions of humans.

The 3Rs framework (Animals (Scientific Procedures) Act 1986):
  • Replace — use alternatives (computer models, cell cultures) where possible
  • Reduce — use the minimum number of animals necessary
  • Refine — minimise suffering; improve procedures to cause less distress

Arguments AGAINST Using Animals

Ethical Concerns Speciesism (Singer, 1975): Discriminating against animals solely because they are not human is morally analogous to racism or sexism. If an animal can suffer, that suffering has moral weight regardless of species. Singer argues the capacity to suffer — not the capacity for reason — is the relevant moral criterion.

Cannot consent: Animals cannot give informed consent, exercise a right to withdraw, or meaningfully communicate distress. The power differential is absolute.

Generalisation limits: Despite evolutionary continuity, rat brains differ from human brains in important ways. Rats cannot model the cognitive, social, cultural and emotional complexity of human addiction — the 'thinking aloud' biases in gambling (Griffiths, 1994) cannot be studied in rats at all.
Evaluation — Balanced Assessment ✔ Olds & Milner's rat research directly led to the mesolimbic reward pathway model, which underpins methadone, naltrexone and antipsychotic drug development — demonstrating real translational value.
✔ The 3Rs framework represents a genuine and enforceable attempt to minimise harm while preserving scientific value — regulated by the Home Office in the UK.
✘ Low ecological validity — laboratory conditions (small cages, artificial stimuli, isolated animals) may produce abnormal behaviour that misrepresents the species' natural responses.
✘ Alternatives are improving — computer modelling, human neuroimaging (PET, fMRI), and in-vitro studies have increasingly reduced (though not eliminated) the need for animal research in psychology.
✘ The argument that animal suffering is justified by human benefit is itself a form of speciesism — it depends on valuing human wellbeing more than animal wellbeing, which Singer argues has no philosophically neutral justification.

Should Psychology be a Science?

One of psychology's most fundamental debates — can and should psychology adopt the methods of natural science, or is human behaviour too complex, subjective and meaningful to be studied scientifically?

The Controversy For science: Psychology should be a natural science — using empirical, objective, quantitative methods (experiments, brain scans, twin studies) to identify causal laws governing behaviour. This gives psychology credibility, reliability and the ability to generate effective treatments.

Against science: Human behaviour involves consciousness, meaning, free will and culture — qualities that cannot be captured by scientific measurement. The scientific method may be inappropriate for psychology, imposing false objectivity on inherently subjective experience. Humanistic and phenomenological approaches argue psychology should study the lived experience of individuals.

What Makes Something 'Scientific'?

Falsifiability (Popper)

A scientific theory must make testable predictions that could in principle be proven wrong. If no evidence could disprove the theory, it is not scientific — it is pseudoscience. Psychoanalytic theory (Freud) is often criticised on this basis: any evidence can be re-interpreted to support it.

Paradigms (Kuhn)

Science progresses through paradigm shifts — when anomalies accumulate, the dominant framework is replaced. Psychology may lack a single unifying paradigm — different approaches (biological, cognitive, behaviourist) compete rather than cohere. Kuhn argued this makes psychology a pre-science.

Objectivity & Replication

Science requires objective measurement and replicable findings. The replication crisis in psychology (Open Science Collaboration, 2015) found that only ~36% of published psychology findings could be successfully replicated — raising serious questions about the scientific rigour of the field.

Empiricism

Knowledge must come from observable, measurable experience — not speculation or intuition. Brain imaging (PET, fMRI) and twin studies have made psychology increasingly empirical, but qualitative methods (interviews, case studies) are harder to reconcile with strict empiricism.

Scientific Methods in Component 3

Examples of Scientific Methods — Supporting the Case FOR Science
PET scans
Volkow et al. (1997): measured D2 receptor density objectively in addiction. Replicable, quantitative, not reliant on self-report. Supports science.
RCTs
Kuipers et al. (1997): randomised controlled trial of CBT for schizophrenia. Gold standard of scientific research design. Supports science.
Twin studies
Kendler et al. (1992): controlled design separating genetic from environmental factors in addiction. High internal validity. Supports science.
Lab experiments
Kiecolt-Glaser et al. (1984): controlled measurement of NK cell activity under stress. Objective biological measure. Supports science.
Limitations of Science in Component 3 — The Case AGAINST
Case studies
Fromm-Reichmann's schizophrenogenic mother theory based on clinical observations — subjective, not replicable, not falsifiable. Questions whether clinical experience counts as science.
Self-report bias
Rahe et al. (1970): relied on self-report of life events. Raphael et al. (1991): only 25% of events appeared on both recall lists — demonstrating unreliability. Not objective science.
Replication crisis
Griffiths (1994) thinking-aloud study — small N (30+30), difficult to replicate exact conditions. Many psychology findings in addiction and stress research have poor replication rates.

Key Perspectives on the Debate

Arguments FOR Psychology as Science

  • Brain imaging produces objective, quantitative, replicable data
  • Experimental methods (RCTs) have produced effective evidence-based treatments (CBT, antipsychotics, methadone)
  • Twin studies allow controlled separation of genetic from environmental factors
  • Scientific status gives psychology credibility — important for funding, policy influence and clinical practice
  • Behaviourism and biological psychology are fully scientific by any criterion

Arguments AGAINST / Limitations

  • The replication crisis — only 36% of psychological findings replicate (OSC, 2015)
  • Laboratory experiments lack ecological validity — artificial conditions may not reflect real-world behaviour
  • Psychoanalytic and humanistic approaches are not falsifiable — important parts of psychology may be inherently unscientific
  • Demand characteristics, social desirability and observer effects undermine objectivity
  • Kuhn: psychology lacks a single paradigm — may still be a pre-science
  • Human consciousness and meaning cannot be reduced to measurable variables without losing what matters most
Evaluation — Reaching a Conclusion ✔ Science has produced genuine progress in understanding stress (HPA axis, immunosuppression), addiction (dopamine pathway, D2 receptors) and schizophrenia (structural abnormalities, antipsychotic effectiveness). Without scientific methods, these discoveries — and the treatments based on them — would not have been possible.
✔ The replication crisis is being addressed — pre-registration of studies, open data sharing, and larger replication projects are improving the reliability of psychological science.
✘ The replication crisis is serious — if most psychology findings don't replicate, the claim to scientific status is significantly weakened. A science that consistently produces non-replicable results is not functioning as science should.
✘ Paradigm issue (Kuhn) — the coexistence of biological, cognitive, behavourist, psychodynamic and humanistic approaches without resolution suggests psychology has not achieved genuine scientific consensus. Each approach uses different methods and reaches different conclusions about the same phenomena.
✘ Humanistic critique — the most important aspects of human experience (love, meaning, suffering, identity) may be inherently resistant to scientific measurement. A psychology that can only study what is measurable may miss what matters most about being human.

Ethical Implications of Psychological Research

Psychological research has real-world consequences — for participants, for the populations studied, and for society.

The Controversy Psychology's pursuit of scientific knowledge can conflict with the rights and welfare of research participants and vulnerable populations. The BPS Code of Ethics (2021) sets out four key principles: Respect, Competence, Responsibility and Integrity. The controversy is whether these principles are always followed, and what happens when scientific value appears to justify compromising participant welfare.

BPS Ethical Guidelines — Key Principles

Informed Consent

Participants must understand what they are agreeing to. Deception is permitted where necessary and followed by a full debrief — but raises questions about genuinely voluntary participation.

Protection from Harm

No greater risk than everyday life. Harder to apply for vulnerable groups — e.g. patients with schizophrenia in acute episodes who may lack capacity to give valid consent to medication or research participation.

Right to Withdraw

Participants may leave at any point without penalty. Problematic in longitudinal studies (Rahe et al. — naval personnel may feel social pressure not to withdraw) and where participants are in institutions.

Confidentiality & Privacy

Data anonymised and not shared beyond the agreed purpose. Increasingly complex when mental health data is held digitally and potentially accessible to third parties.

Ethical Issues Specific to Component 3

Kiecolt-Glaser et al. (1984) — Stress Research
Blood samples from medical students — minimal harm, but students may have felt unable to refuse participation from authority figures. Knowing their immune function was being tested could itself induce stress — a confounding variable and ethical concern.
Olds & Milner (1954) — Addiction Research
Invasive brain surgery on rats without consent. Today requires Home Office approval under the Animals (Scientific Procedures) Act 1986. The knowledge gained (mesolimbic reward pathway) has been enormously valuable — but raises whether any scientific benefit justifies this level of harm to a sentient being.
Antipsychotic Medication — Schizophrenia
Patients in acute psychotic episodes may lack capacity for valid informed consent. The Mental Health Act allows compulsory treatment. Szasz (1960) — the 'chemical straitjacket' argument: antipsychotics may be used for social control rather than genuine therapeutic benefit. Tiihonen et al. (2006): 37-fold increase in suicide when medication is stopped — yet compliance is only 54% (Rettenbacher et al.). The tension between autonomy and duty of care is acute.
Aversion Therapy — Addiction
Deliberately causing nausea, physical distress or discomfort (Antabuse, rapid smoking) raises significant harm concerns. Requires fully informed, voluntary consent — problematic when working with addicted individuals who may agree under duress or desperation. Fuller et al. (1986): even when it reduces drinking frequency, abstinence rates are similar to placebo — raising whether causing harm for limited benefit is justified.

Socially Sensitive Research

Research findings can be used in ways researchers did not intend. Sieber & Stanley (1988) defined socially sensitive research as any study with potential social consequences for participants or the group they represent.

Genetic addiction research (Blum et al., 1990; Kendler et al., 1992): identifying biological vulnerabilities in individuals or groups could enable discrimination by employers or insurers. Could be used to label individuals as 'at risk' before any problematic behaviour.

Cultural bias research (Harrison et al., 1988): highlighting over-diagnosis of schizophrenia in African-Caribbean communities could be used to stereotype or stigmatise, rather than improve diagnostic fairness.

Psychologists bear responsibility not just for how they conduct research but for how their findings are subsequently used and reported in media — yet this is very difficult to control once findings are published.
Evaluation ✔ BPS guidelines and ethics committees (IRBs) have dramatically improved research ethics since the 1970s — landmark unethical studies (e.g. the Stanford Prison Experiment) could not receive ethical approval today.
✔ The debrief procedure is a practical, principled compromise — allowing deception where scientifically necessary while minimising lasting harm and respecting participant dignity post-study.
✘ Socially sensitive research is unavoidable — some of the most important research in mental health (on addiction genetics, diagnostic bias, schizophrenia relapse) is inherently sensitive. Refusing to conduct socially sensitive research would impoverish the field without eliminating the sensitivity.
✘ Vulnerable populations require additional safeguards that existing guidelines do not always provide. The line between treatment and research participation is often blurred in clinical psychology, and power differentials between clinician-researcher and patient-participant are difficult to fully eliminate.
✘ BPS guidelines reflect Western individualist values — emphasis on individual informed consent may be inappropriate in collectivist cultures where community consent is the relevant ethical norm. The framework is not truly universal.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1 — Description (10 marks)

Non-human animal research has played a central role in establishing some of psychology's most important theories, including the mesolimbic dopamine reward pathway that underlies our understanding of addiction. Olds and Milner (1954) implanted electrodes in the nucleus accumbens of rats, allowing them to self-stimulate the reward pathway by pressing a lever. The rats pressed the lever up to 7,000 times per hour, choosing brain stimulation over food and water even when starved. This provided the foundational evidence that the mesolimbic dopamine pathway is the biological basis of reward — and by extension, that addictive substances hijack this same pathway to produce artificially amplified pleasure signals.

McMurran et al. (2010) used impulsive and non-impulsive rats (identified through premature responding on a task) and found that impulsive rats were significantly more likely to self-administer cocaine. This provided a controlled, causal demonstration of the impulsivity–addiction link — a relationship that is extremely difficult to establish causally in human research due to ethical constraints.

The use of animals in psychological research is governed in the UK by the Animals (Scientific Procedures) Act 1986, which requires researchers to follow the 3Rs: Replace animal studies with alternatives where possible; Reduce the number of animals to the minimum necessary; and Refine procedures to minimise suffering. This requires Home Office approval and ethical oversight before any animal study proceeds.

However, Peter Singer (1975) challenged the moral basis of animal research through his concept of speciesism — the assumption that one species (humans) has greater moral worth than another merely because of species membership. Singer argued that if an animal can suffer, that suffering has moral weight regardless of species — making the standard justification of animal research (human benefit outweighs animal cost) a form of unjustified discrimination. Animals cannot give informed consent, exercise a right to withdraw, or meaningfully communicate their distress — fundamental ethical principles that are simply set aside in animal research.

AO3 — Evaluation (15 marks)

A key argument in favour of justifying animal research is its translational value. Olds and Milner's discovery of the mesolimbic reward pathway directly enabled the development of methadone (an opioid agonist that mimics heroin at the same receptors) and naltrexone (an opioid antagonist that blocks the reward effect). NICE (2007) assessed 31 systematic reviews and found methadone significantly better than placebo for reducing heroin use and criminal activity. Without the animal research, this treatment — which has demonstrably saved lives and reduced harm — would not have been developed. This provides a compelling utilitarian justification: the animal suffering was finite; the human benefit ongoing.

The 3Rs framework further supports the view that animal research can be conducted justifiably. By legally requiring researchers to minimise animal numbers and suffering, and to seek alternatives first, the framework acknowledges the moral weight of animal welfare whilst allowing research where it remains the only viable option. This represents a principled rather than an absolutist position — neither ignoring animal suffering nor prohibiting all research regardless of value.

However, the statement's claim gains force from significant limitations on what animal research can actually demonstrate. Laboratory conditions — small cages, isolated rats, artificial stimuli — have low ecological validity and may produce behaviour that misrepresents the species' natural responses to reward and addiction. More fundamentally, McMurran's impulsive rat model cannot capture the cognitive, social and cultural dimensions of human addiction. Griffiths' (1994) 'thinking aloud' study of cognitive biases in gambling — irrational verbalisations such as 'the machine is in a good mood today' — is simply impossible to replicate in an animal model. The most distinctively human aspects of addiction cannot be studied in animals at all.

Singer's speciesism argument also presents a philosophical challenge that the 3Rs does not fully resolve. The premise that human benefit can justify animal suffering is itself a moral position requiring justification — one that a consistent utilitarian should examine rather than assume. If animal suffering matters morally (which the 3Rs implicitly acknowledges by requiring its minimisation), the question of how much human benefit is needed to outweigh it cannot be answered by regulation alone.

In conclusion, the statement is partially valid but too absolute. Animal research can be justified in specific contexts where its translational value is clear and alternatives are unavailable — as with Olds and Milner's work. However, 'never fully justified' captures something important: no animal study is ethically cost-free, and the burden of justification should be high, ongoing, and subject to the strongest possible regulatory scrutiny.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1 — Description (10 marks)

Science is typically characterised by several key features. Karl Popper proposed that a scientific theory must be falsifiable — it must make predictions that could in principle be proven wrong by evidence. Thomas Kuhn argued that mature sciences operate within a shared paradigm — a framework of assumptions and methods accepted by the scientific community — progressing through revolutionary paradigm shifts when anomalies accumulate. Scientific research is also characterised by empiricism (knowledge from observable evidence), objectivity (findings are independent of the observer) and replicability (findings can be reproduced by independent researchers).

Component 3 contains research that meets these criteria strongly. Volkow et al. (1997) used PET brain imaging to directly measure D2 receptor density in the nucleus accumbens of cocaine, heroin and alcohol addicts — providing objective, quantitative data replicated across multiple independent laboratories. Kiecolt-Glaser et al. (1984) used a controlled quasi-experiment measuring Natural Killer cell activity before and during examinations — objective biological measurement not reliant on self-report. Kendler et al. (1992) used a twin study design to estimate heritability of alcohol dependence — a controlled methodology specifically designed to separate genetic from environmental factors. Kuipers et al. (1997) conducted a randomised controlled trial (RCT) of CBT for schizophrenia — the gold standard design in medicine.

However, Component 3 also contains research that is less scientific. Rahe et al.'s (1970) SRRS study relied entirely on self-report of life events — Raphael et al. (1991) found only 25% recall consistency, suggesting extremely low reliability. Fromm-Reichmann's schizophrenogenic mother theory is based on clinical observation and psychoanalytic interpretation — it is not falsifiable because any evidence can be re-interpreted to support the theory. The replication crisis (Open Science Collaboration, 2015) found that only 36% of published psychology findings could be successfully replicated — raising serious questions about the field's scientific reliability more broadly.

AO3 — Evaluation (15 marks)

A strong argument that psychology can and should be a science comes from the genuine achievements of its most rigorous sub-fields. PET imaging of the dopamine system has produced replicable, clinically useful knowledge — Volkow et al.'s D2 findings have been independently replicated and directly inform pharmacological addiction treatment (methadone, naltrexone). NICE's (2007) systematic reviews of methadone and Kuipers et al.'s RCT of CBT demonstrate that psychology can meet the same evidential standards as medicine. Scientific status also has practical value: it gives psychology credibility with policymakers, secures research funding, and means that evidence-based guidelines (NICE, NHS) can mandate treatments like CBT for schizophrenia.

Furthermore, the replication crisis — whilst serious — is being actively addressed. Pre-registration of study designs, open data sharing, and large-scale replication projects are improving transparency and reducing publication bias. The crisis may reflect a period of methodological reform rather than a permanent indictment of psychology as non-scientific.

However, Kuhn's paradigm analysis presents a genuine challenge. Unlike physics or chemistry, psychology does not operate within a single shared paradigm. Biological, cognitive, behaviourist, psychodynamic and humanistic approaches coexist — each using different methods, making different assumptions, and reaching different conclusions about the same phenomena. In the study of schizophrenia, for example, the biological approach (dopamine hypothesis) and the psychodynamic approach (schizophrenogenic mother) are not just different theories — they are based on entirely different epistemological commitments. This paradigm plurality is characteristic of what Kuhn called a pre-science.

The replication crisis itself is more damaging than reformists acknowledge. If only 36% of published findings replicate, the field has a fundamental problem with objectivity and the reliability of its knowledge base — not merely a methodological one. Furthermore, psychoanalytic and humanistic approaches — which address some of the most important human experiences (meaning, suffering, relationships) — are inherently unfalsifiable by Popper's criterion. Excluding them from psychology would impoverish the discipline; including them makes it epistemologically mixed rather than purely scientific.

In conclusion, the question is not whether psychology should be a science, but whether it can be uniformly one. Its most rigorous sub-fields (neuropsychology, psychopharmacology, clinical trials methodology) are genuinely scientific and produce reliable, replicable knowledge. However, as a whole discipline psychology is epistemologically plural — and this may be not a failure, but an appropriate reflection of the complexity and diversity of its subject matter: human behaviour and experience.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1 — Description (10 marks)

Cultural bias in psychology refers to the tendency for psychological research to reflect the norms, values and assumptions of a particular culture — typically Western — and to present findings derived from that culture as universally applicable. Ethnocentrism involves judging or interpreting other cultures through the lens of one's own cultural norms. Beta bias refers to minimising or ignoring cultural differences — applying WEIRD findings to non-Western populations without justification. Alpha bias involves exaggerating cultural differences, treating cultures as monolithic rather than acknowledging internal variation.

Henrich et al. (2010) documented the scale of the problem: 96% of research published in the leading psychology journals used Western samples, and 68% used American samples specifically — yet Western populations represent only approximately 12% of the world's total population. Any conclusion drawn from such samples carries very limited claim to universality.

Component 3 provides concrete examples. Yan-Ping Zheng and Keh-Ming Lin (1994) administered Holmes and Rahe's Social Readjustment Rating Scale (SRRS, 1967) to a Chinese sample and found significantly different LCU ratings for the same life events compared to the original American norms. Events involving interpersonal shame and loss of face were rated far more stressful — demonstrating that the scale encodes American cultural values and cannot be validly applied cross-culturally without modification. Harrison et al. (1988) found that African-Caribbean individuals were diagnosed with schizophrenia at disproportionately higher rates than white British individuals in Nottingham, suggesting that diagnostic systems developed in Western cultural contexts may misclassify culturally-specific expressions of distress — ethnocentrism in clinical practice. Becker et al. (2002) found that following the introduction of Western television to Fiji in 1995, rates of disordered eating in adolescent girls rose dramatically — demonstrating that Western media norms can penetrate and distort behaviour in non-Western cultures, but also highlighting the cultural specificity of much media-effects research.

AO3 — Evaluation (15 marks)

Cultural bias critiques have driven genuine improvements in psychological practice. DSM-5 now includes a Cultural Formulation Interview, designed to assess the cultural context of a patient's presentation before diagnosis — a direct response to the Harrison et al. findings on over-diagnosis. NICE guidelines increasingly include cultural sensitivity considerations. These changes suggest the field is capable of self-correction in response to cultural bias critiques, which is a mark of scientific maturity.

Furthermore, cross-cultural research has demonstrated that not all of psychology's findings are culturally specific. David Buss (1989) studied mate preferences across 37 cultures and found consistent patterns — such as the universal preference for healthy partners and the cross-cultural sex difference in valuing resource acquisition versus physical attractiveness. Some biological responses (such as the HPA axis stress response and its cortisol-mediated immunosuppression) appear physiologically universal. This suggests cultural bias does not invalidate all psychological findings — rather, it requires researchers to specify the scope of their conclusions more carefully.

However, the WEIRD problem documented by Henrich et al. remains deeply embedded in the structure of academic psychology. Elite journals, prestigious universities and major funding bodies are disproportionately located in the United States and Western Europe. Even researchers who are aware of cultural bias face practical constraints: global representative sampling is logistically difficult and expensive. Awareness of the problem does not solve it.

Cultural relativism also has limits. Taken to its logical extreme, it would mean that no behaviour can be assessed cross-culturally — including practices that cause genuine harm. Some psychologists argue that universal psychological needs and rights transcend cultural context, and that a psychology which cannot make any cross-cultural judgements has abandoned a core purpose. Finally, treating 'culture' as a single explanatory variable risks alpha bias — within any given culture, individual differences in personality, socioeconomic status and experience may be far larger than differences between cultures, making cultural explanations their own kind of oversimplification.

In conclusion, cultural bias is a genuine and serious limitation of much psychological research. It does not invalidate findings but significantly restricts their generalisability. The discipline's response — more culturally sensitive diagnostic tools, cross-cultural replication, and explicit acknowledgement of sampling limitations — represents progress, though the structural barriers to truly representative sampling remain substantial.

Band 4 Model Answer
AO1 — Description (10 marks)

The British Psychological Society's Code of Ethics (2021) sets out principles that govern psychological research: informed consent (participants must understand what they are agreeing to); protection from harm (no greater risk than in everyday life); right to withdraw (without penalty at any point); and confidentiality (data anonymised and not shared beyond the agreed purpose). Where deception is necessary for scientific validity, it must be justified and followed by a full debrief.

Olds and Milner's (1954) research on the mesolimbic reward pathway raises fundamental ethical concerns in relation to animal participants. Rats underwent invasive brain surgery to have electrodes implanted in the nucleus accumbens — a procedure involving significant physical harm. Animals cannot give informed consent, exercise a right to withdraw, or express their distress in ways that can be reliably interpreted. Every core BPS principle was therefore inapplicable to this research by definition.

The use of antipsychotic medication with patients diagnosed with schizophrenia raises distinct but equally significant ethical issues. Patients in acute psychotic episodes may lack the capacity to give valid informed consent to medication. The Mental Health Act allows compulsory treatment — but Szasz (1960) argued this represented a 'chemical straitjacket': medication used for social control and compliance rather than genuine therapeutic benefit. The tension is sharpened by the data: Rettenbacher et al. found full compliance in only 54.2% of patients, whilst Tiihonen et al. (2006) found a 37-fold increase in suicide risk when medication was stopped. These figures suggest that neither forced treatment nor non-compliance is without serious harm — a genuinely irresolvable ethical tension.

Sieber and Stanley (1988) identified a further ethical dimension — socially sensitive research: studies with potential social consequences for participants or the groups they represent. Research on genetic vulnerability to addiction (Blum et al., 1990; Kendler et al., 1992) identifies biological risk markers that could be used by employers or insurers to discriminate against individuals before any problematic behaviour has occurred. Psychologists cannot control how their findings are used once published.

AO3 — Evaluation (15 marks)

A significant strength is that ethics committees (IRBs) and the BPS Code have dramatically raised standards since the 1970s. Studies that are now considered landmark examples of unethical research — such as Milgram's (1963) obedience experiments or the Stanford Prison Experiment — could not receive ethical approval today. The systematic scrutiny of research proposals before they begin represents a genuine structural improvement in research ethics across psychology.

The debrief procedure also represents a principled compromise — allowing deception where it is scientifically necessary (as in some stress and addiction research where full disclosure would eliminate the phenomenon being studied) whilst respecting participant dignity and restoring full understanding after participation. This balance enables important research to proceed without lasting harm to participants.

However, vulnerable populations present a persistent challenge that existing guidelines do not fully resolve. Patients with schizophrenia in acute episodes, individuals in the grip of serious addiction, and people under severe stress may all lack the psychological resources to give genuinely free consent. The power differential between clinician-researcher and patient-participant is very difficult to eliminate — patients in institutional settings may fear that declining participation will affect their treatment. The line between therapeutic intervention and research participation is often blurred in clinical psychology, creating complex and unresolved consent issues.

The problem of socially sensitive research is similarly difficult to resolve within existing frameworks. Genetic research on addiction identifies biological vulnerabilities that carry significant social risks — yet the research also has the potential to inform more targeted, effective prevention and treatment. Psychologists bear a responsibility not just for how they conduct research but for how they communicate findings and anticipate their social use — a responsibility that publication norms and ethical review boards currently address only weakly. Finally, the BPS framework reflects Western individualist values: its emphasis on individual informed consent may not translate to collectivist cultural contexts where community consent is the more appropriate ethical norm.

In conclusion, ethical standards in psychological research have improved substantially, but genuine tensions remain — particularly around vulnerable populations, the limits of informed consent in clinical contexts, and the social consequences of sensitive research findings. Ethics is not a problem that can be fully solved by guidelines; it requires ongoing, context-specific judgement throughout the research process.
